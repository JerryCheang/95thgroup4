Executive Summary

Celebrating the school's 95th year, the new Building D, dubbed the Vertical Eco-Life Hub, turns environmental education from textbook-based theory into an immersive, hands-on experience. Embodying sustainable development, it presents a forward-thinking green model for the entire community by directly confronting the urgent challenges of climate change. Functioning like a "growing and breathing" living system, the centre uses innovative mycelium bricks to reduce construction waste. It also employs advanced filtration to purify wastewater, recycling it for toilets and irrigation. This dynamic space is a living ecology textbook, perfectly illustrating circular resource flows. It provides a unique, practical learning experience that powerfully bridges the gap between theory and real-world application, inspiring active participation in environmental stewardship.

Mycelium Bricks (SDG 12)

The building walls are constructed entirely from mycelium bricks, fully aligned with Sustainable Development Goal 12. These biodegradable, bio-based materials replace conventional concrete and brick, reducing both carbon-intensive manufacturing and raw material extraction.

Compared with traditional fired clay or concrete production, mycelium bricks require significantly less energy. At end-of-life, they naturally decompose and return nutrients to the soil, creating a closed-loop lifecycle that lowers landfill waste and reduces reliance on fossil fuel-based construction inputs.

Rooftop Eco-Garden (SDG 11)

The rooftop ecological garden is designed as an inclusive, welcoming green public space. During school hours, it serves as an outdoor classroom for ecosystem observation. During leisure time, it is open to residents, including those with pets, supporting social inclusion and community harmony.

This transforms underused rooftop space into urban ecological capacity, improves microclimate, and supports sustainable urban planning goals under SDG 11.

The garden also uses an advanced wastewater recycling system for irrigation and non-potable toilet flushing. With multi-stage treatment, including sedimentation and biofiltration, it closes the campus water cycle, increases reuse rate, and reduces freshwater demand across daily campus operation.

Underground Parking Lot (SDG 7)

The underground car park advances SDG 7 by integrating visible EV charging stations connected to the campus renewable energy framework, including potential rooftop solar supply.

By placing clean transport infrastructure in a high-use campus facility, the design lowers barriers to low-carbon commuting, improves convenience, and reduces dependence on off-campus charging and fossil-fuel vehicles. The parking system therefore acts as an active clean energy deployment node rather than a passive support facility.

Sustainability Features

Smart Design: Bring-Your-Own Tableware Points Card System (Tuck Shop)

At the beginning of each school year, every student receives an exclusive physical points card from their homeroom teacher. When students bring non-disposable tableware, such as lunch boxes or cups, to buy meals at the canteen, they present both the tableware and card at checkout.

The cashier verifies that the tableware is reusable and then stamps or adds points using a dedicated card reader. Points are earned only through the use of personal reusable tableware, never expire, and can be accumulated long-term.

Students can redeem rewards at the points redemption zone, including notebooks, custom eco-friendly bags, small potted plants, and portable tableware sets. This points-for-gifts mechanism creates tangible incentives for eco-friendly action, helping reduce disposable tableware consumption and supporting school environmental goals.

Green Technologies

Sewage Recycling System

In daily campus life, rainwater and handwashing water are often discharged directly to sewers, wasting recyclable water resources. This project converts that wastewater into reusable water for toilet flushing and rooftop irrigation.

Wastewater is first collected in a storage tank and then directed to an adjacent filtration tank. The screen removes visible impurities such as leaves and large suspended solids. Water then passes through the core filter media layer, including quartz sand that captures fine particles through narrow pathways.

After comprehensive treatment, the water is converted into clean reclaimed water and stored in a clean water tank, ready for reuse.

Mycelium-Based Wall Materials (Mycelium Wall)

The building's interior and exterior walls use mycelium-based composites, a renewable and biodegradable alternative to traditional concrete and brick. These materials are grown by cultivating fungal mycelium on agricultural by-products such as straw and corn stalks.

At end-of-life, mycelium bricks can be fully composted, diverting industrial waste from landfills and closing the material loop. They also reduce dependence on cement-heavy construction and repurpose agricultural waste that would otherwise be burned.

In addition, their porous structure naturally regulates humidity by absorbing excess moisture in humid conditions and releasing it in drier periods.

Data from Macau's Transport Bureau (Figure 12 and Figure 13) shows that as of 2023, Macau had 250,246 registered motor vehicles, while new energy vehicles (electric, natural gas, and hybrid vehicles) totaled only 13,838. Traditional vehicles still dominate, and new energy vehicles account for only a small share.

This directly supports the plan to integrate EV charging stations into the underground car park. Convenient charging access helps reduce charging anxiety, lowers barriers to adoption, and encourages both campus users and the wider community to transition from traditional vehicles to cleaner alternatives.

Green Space Coverage and Number of Parks

Comparative data shows a sharp contrast between Hong Kong and Macau. Hong Kong has rich green reserves, with over 40% green coverage. Macau has a much smaller land area (about 33 km2), high population density, and green space coverage of about 21.5%.

Most green spaces in Macau are fragmented and small in scale, with 44 parks and gardens in total. This suggests current green spaces are not sufficient to fully meet public needs. Therefore, the new Block D sky garden is planned as an additional green area open to the public on weekends.
